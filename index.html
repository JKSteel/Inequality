Why the rich keep getting richer

Picture a population of households, each starting with a different amount of wealth and sharing one economy. Every household has its own run of good and bad luck over time. Now replay that exact population twice under near-identical conditions — the same households with the same starting wealth and the same run of luck — changing one thing only: how much wealth is redistributed each year. What do you think happens?

Model assumptions

Key (what the variables mean and where the numbers come from)

m, economic growth
Wealth growth rate per year. For developed countries, 2%/yr is roughly the long-run growth in real GDP per person. Both panels use the same m; redistribution rate doesn't affect growth rate, which is well supported by the historical record [1].
σ, volatility (luck)
How much the returns vary from year to year. This is the part of a household's outcome that's luck, not skill. Calvet, Campbell, & Sodini (2007) [2] tracked every Swedish household's portfolio and found a volatility of 20%. Intelligence and hard work can lift your average return, but neither change the variance.
J, redistribution
How much wealth is moved each year from those above average wealth to those below it, through taxes, transfers, public spending, and the like. Note that J is not itself a tax rate.

In practice, J is a dependent variable. The model's long-run shape follows a Pareto distribution with exponent μ = 1 + J/σ2, where μ and σ are empirically measured. Today, μ has been measured to be approximately 1.5 [3], while μ of the "golden age" (1950s-70s) was around 2.0 [4]. These values of μ imply J values of approximately 2% today and 4% in the mid-century.

There is a critical J value above which the wealth distribution stabilises and below which it keeps concentrating at the top. This critical point depends solely on the volatility, which with the empirical value above gives Jc = 4%. Today, we sit below this point (J < σ2); the mid-century is roughly at the threshold.

"Typical" means the median household, not the average/mean, which the very rich skew upward.

Why the two economy sizes (pies) differ. Redistribution shuffles wealth around, it doesn't make or destroy any by itself. However, the two pies can slowly drift apart because growth is multiplicative: once a few hold most of it, the whole economy's growth rides on them, including their luck, and the extra volatility drags the pie down a touch compared with the more equal one.

Real-world approximation. Adds wealth-dependent returns: richer households earn higher returns of approximately 2.8 percentage points more per year per order of magnitude of wealth [5]. This is modelled as a pure transfer: total wealth is conserved each step, so what the wealthy gain in dollar terms comes directly out of everyone else's pockets, not from new growth. This is the most conservative framing possible and is motivated by Rognlie (2016) [6] (the entire rise in net capital income since 1948 is from housing/land, inherently zero-sum) and Stiglitz (2012) [7] (excess returns reflect rent-seeking, not wealth creation).

If simulating the model beyond a normal lifetime, read each household as a family line and the wealth as inherited.

What this is. The Bouchaud-;Mézard model [8] is a physics-of-economics (econophysics) model about what happens to wealth when it compounds and is subject to volatility (i.e. luck). Both panels follow the same households in the same economy, with each household dealt the same run of luck in both. The only difference between them is J: how much wealth gets redistributed each year.

  • Mechanism. Wealth compounds equally for every household, but each year's growth carries a random element from volatility — some good years, some bad. Because that growth applies to what you already own, a bigger pile means a bigger swing, and lucky streaks snowball.
  • Result. Leave the system alone and wealth inequality increases; a smaller and smaller group ends up with almost everything. Redistribute as much as the mid-century did and it holds at a far healthier spread instead. That era sat right on the critical line J = σ2 (the boundary below which wealth runs away to the top) so even "strong" redistribution here is only just enough to hold the line, not comfortably past it.
  • Takeaway. Redistribution of wealth doesn't shrink the economy, as historical data has shown. Both systems grow overall, and the low-redistribution side grows no faster than the high-redistribution side (if anything it's usually a bit slower). What changes is who gets the growth. The value of the whole economy pulls away from the typical household: the pie grows, the median gets left behind. This is because most wealth is funnelled to the top.
